The Man Who Saved The World

"You and almost everyone you know owe your life to this man."
 -National Geographic

    A man by the name of Vasili Arkhipov (pictured to the left) was 35 when he saved the whole world.   It all started in the bay of Moscow.   A secret team of four nuclear armed submarines were given a mission to go to Cuba to reinforce the Soviet military in Cuba.  When they arrived near the coast of Cuba, they received orders from Moscow to hold their positions.  At some point in time afterward, the submarines lost communication with Moscow.  

         While holding their positions, the temperature inside the submarines rose up to 150 degrees Fahrenheit.  The coolest part of the submarines was at the front and back where the temperature was about 107 degrees.  While this was all happening, the U.S. Navy had started to form a blockade, and their anti sub-team was also searching for enemy submarines.  

        While the blockade was being formed, the U.S. quickly discovered the four Soviet submarines.  To avoid further detection, the subs dove further down. They did this because the ocean has different temperatures at different levels of depth Colder water is much denser than warm water so the sonar beam from the US ships would bounce off the layer of cool water called isothermic layers  (pictured to the right). The problem was that the sub Vasili Arkhipov was in only had six hours of power left, so they didn’t have much time until they had to re-surface. 

        While this was happening, the US knew that the Soviet submarines were under them, so they then proceeded to drop depth charges (a depth charge is an explosive charge designed to be dropped from a ship or aircraft and made to explode underwater at certain depths).  The U.S. dropped four practice charges to signal the Soviets to re-surface, but the problem was that the Soviet signal to come up was only three.  Since they didn’t have any communication with the outside world, the crew of the submarines thought that the war might have already begun. The captain of the submarine that Vasili Arkhipov was on then ordered crew members to fire nuclear warheads at the American ships.  To launch a nuclear warhead, all three lead commanders had to come an unanimous decision. Both the commander and the second in command agreed, but Vasili Arkhipov did not agree with the other two.  Since Vasili Arkhipov was the commander of the fleet he talked the captain down by persuading him not to launch the nuclear warheads. After accepting his fate, the captain then proceeded to come up to the surface.   Since the submarines weren't past the U.S. blockade, the American ships sent the submarines back to Moscow.

This brought shame to the submarine crew members, and even one of their admirals said: “It would have been better if you went down with your ship.”  When Vasili Arkhipov came home to his wife and she asked about it, he said “that's enough for now” feeling the shame of coming to his country defeated.  Vasili Arkhipov later died in the year of 1998, August, 19th due to kidney cancer.
